Type 2 Diabetes

Chronic metabolic disorder affecting blood sugar regulation

ICD-10: E11
Endocrine Disorder
Chronic Condition
Last updated: December 20, 2025
Medically reviewed by Dr. Sarah Johnson, MD
8 min read

What is Type 2 Diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by high blood sugar levels due to the body's inability to effectively use insulin or produce enough insulin. Unlike Type 1 diabetes, which is typically diagnosed in childhood, Type 2 diabetes usually develops in adults and is often linked to lifestyle factors.

Key Facts

  • Affects over 400 million people worldwide
  • Accounts for 90-95% of all diabetes cases
  • Often preventable through lifestyle modifications
  • Can be effectively managed with proper care

The condition develops when the pancreas doesn't produce enough insulin or when the body becomes resistant to insulin's effects. Insulin is essential for allowing glucose to enter cells and be used for energy.

Signs and Symptoms

Type 2 diabetes symptoms often develop gradually and may be subtle initially. Many people have the condition for years before being diagnosed.

Common Symptoms

Increased Thirst

Excessive thirst (polydipsia) due to high blood sugar levels

Frequent Urination

Increased urination (polyuria), especially at night

Fatigue

Persistent tiredness and lack of energy

Blurred Vision

Vision problems due to high blood sugar affecting the eyes

Seek Immediate Medical Attention if you experience these symptoms.

Slow-healing wounds

Cuts and bruises that take longer than usual to heal

Frequent infections

Recurring skin, gum, or urinary tract infections

Unexplained weight loss

Losing weight without trying

Numbness or tingling

In hands or feet (diabetic neuropathy)

Causes and Risk Factors

Type 2 diabetes develops due to a combination of genetic and lifestyle factors. Understanding these can help in prevention and management.

Primary Mechanisms

1

Insulin Resistance

Body cells become less responsive to insulin, requiring more insulin to maintain normal blood sugar levels.

2

Beta Cell Dysfunction

Pancreatic beta cells gradually lose their ability to produce sufficient insulin.

Risk Factors

Modifiable (You Can Control)

Obesity and excess weight
Physical inactivity
Poor diet high in processed foods
Smoking

Non-Modifiable (Fixed Factors)

Age (45 years and older)
Family history of diabetes
Ethnicity (higher risk in certain groups)
History of gestational diabetes

Treatment and Management

Type 2 diabetes management focuses on maintaining healthy blood sugar levels through a comprehensive approach combining lifestyle modifications, monitoring, and when necessary, medications.

Lifestyle Changes

Diet, exercise, and healthy habits form the foundation of diabetes management.

Blood Sugar Monitoring

Regular monitoring helps track progress and adjust treatment plans.

Medications

Various medications can help manage blood sugar when lifestyle changes aren't sufficient.

Detailed Treatment Approaches

Dietary Changes
  • Focus on whole grains, vegetables, and lean proteins
  • Limit refined sugars and processed foods
  • Control portion sizes
  • Eat regular, balanced meals
Physical Activity
  • Aim for 150 minutes of moderate exercise weekly
  • Include both aerobic and resistance training
  • Start slowly and gradually increase intensity
  • Monitor blood sugar before and after exercise
Important: All medications should be prescribed and monitored by healthcare professionals. Never start or stop medications without medical supervision.
First-Line Medications
Metformin

Reduces glucose production by the liver and improves insulin sensitivity

Additional Options
Sulfonylureas

Stimulate insulin production from pancreatic beta cells

GLP-1 Agonists

Help regulate blood sugar and may promote weight loss

Prevention Strategies

Good News!

Type 2 diabetes is largely preventable. Studies show that lifestyle changes can reduce the risk of developing Type 2 diabetes by up to 58% in high-risk individuals.

Primary Prevention

Maintain Healthy Weight

Even a 5-10% weight loss can significantly reduce diabetes risk

Eat a Balanced Diet

Focus on whole foods, fiber-rich vegetables, and lean proteins

Stay Physically Active

Regular exercise improves insulin sensitivity and glucose metabolism

Regular Screening

Who Should Get Tested?

  • Adults 45 years and older
  • People with risk factors at any age
  • Women with history of gestational diabetes

Common Tests

  • Fasting blood glucose test
  • HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin) test
  • Oral glucose tolerance test

When to Seek Medical Care

Seek Immediate Emergency Care

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity breath odor, rapid breathing

Severe Hypoglycemia

Confusion, seizures, loss of consciousness, inability to swallow

Extremely High Blood Sugar

Blood glucose over 400 mg/dL with severe dehydration

Emergency Contacts

Schedule Regular Appointments

Routine Diabetes Care

Every 3-6 months for blood sugar monitoring and medication adjustments

Preventive Screenings

Annual eye exams, foot checks, kidney function tests

Diabetes Education

Work with certified diabetes educators for comprehensive care
